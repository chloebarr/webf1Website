Literature Review

Mitigating personal information exposure on the web

This literature review studies the topic of mitigating personal information exposure on the web. This is considered an important topic because increasingly more of the world population has access to the internet with minimal education on how our personal information is used and how to keep it safe. When conducting research the three topics found to be the most relevant when mitigating personal information exposure on the web were: why and how we disclose personal information, how to manage personal information and how to know when our personal information is being used.

Why and how we disclose personal information:

When looking at how web users disclose their data, several studies had ideas on how the web is used and why anyone would disclose their data online. Typical use of the internet includes consuming media, shopping and building relationships online daily (Kim and Kim, 2018, p.1; Chen, Chen and Lam 2008 ). The value and importance of data is increasing, (Kim and Kim, 2018, p.1) with many people often feeling pressure to disclose personal data online for various reasons, such as an obligation to be a member of a website ( Chouseinoglou, Ög˘ütçü, and Testik, 2015). The reluctance of consumers to provide personal information is increased when they feel they cannot control the privacy risk (Kim and Kim, 2018, p.3). As the value of data increases, more companies will try to gain internet users data. These companies will spend lots of money on marketing techniques to get this data, internet users are often lured in.

Several of studies have looked at the ranking of self-disclosed data on social media sites, self-disclosed data is data that is provided by the user. The first study by Lam, Chen, and Chen concluded the order was (in order of most disclosed): nickname, gender, birthday, introduction, education, blood, email, height, occupation, yahoo id, MSN id, weight and skype id (2008, p.5) this data is however limited to the social media platform Wretch. A study by Acquisti and Gross found that the order of data disclosed on Facebook was different: Name, Profile image, birthday, high school, AIM screen name, hometown, relationship status, music/movie preference, interests, relationship partner (2005, p.5). Birthday data would appear to be most common, birthday data on Facebook allows other users to post birthday messages on the users' profile, meaning the incentive to give a fake birthday is unclear (Acquisti and Gross, 2005, p.6). Having lots of personal information such as birthdays online leaves people open to identity fraud, “Identity theft is one of the fastest growing white-collar crimes…”(Griffiths, 2003).

In the same study performed by Lam, Chen and Chen they set out to prove that even if users do not expose personal information online, others may for them (2008, p.1) Free form text on the social media platform Wretch allowed users to input text about each other. From this text, the study managed to infer 72% of the users' first names (2008, p.8).

In 2015, 4.15 billion people had a mobile phone (Statista, 2016). Mobile phones contain lots of personal information, due to the number of applications upon them (Bakopoulou, Choffnes, Le, Markopoulou, Mehrabadi and Shuba, 2018).Lots of papers agree that sometimes the transmission of Personal information is justified, such as location information when using the application Google maps. However, they all also agree that there can be a malicious side to sharing personal data, such as stalking and harassment. (Bakopoulou, Choffnes, Le, Markopoulou, Mehrabadi and Shuba, 2018; Kim and Kim, 2018; Lam, Chen & Chen, 2008 ). Mobile phones should be used with caution as they have the power to share personal information without the users' knowledge.

How to know when our personal information is being used:

For the most part web users do not know how their personal information is shared and for what purpose, they do not have control over their data (Bakopoulou, Choffnes, Le, Markopoulou, Mehrabadi and Shuba, 2018). As there is ever increasing data online, one way to make data more relevant for the user is through personal recommendation systems. Recommendation systems recommend data based on objective behaviour or explicitly stated preferences . Although accumulated data may help users, they may also feel uncomfortable being monitored(Kim and Kim, 2018).

Bakopoulou Et Al. paper details their system for a secure and efficient on-device packet level monitoring and detection of sensitive information. This system would show all users of applications if the application transmits personal information over a network, and where personal information is being sent to (2018). If users are given more information about where their data is going then they can make more informed decisions on which applications to keep on their phone.

How to manage our personal information:

Many people are using the internet, the world bank reports that as of 2013, 38.1% of the world population is using the internet, whereas 42% of the households own a computer(World bank, 2014, cited in Chouseinoglou, Ög˘ütçü and Testik, 2015, p.1). Despite this level of technology individuals behaviours with regard to protecting their own privacy have not progressed (Chouseinoglou, Ög˘ütçü, and Testik, 2015), with many users downloading data and applications by default (Bakopoulou, Choffnes, Le, Markopoulou, Mehrabadi and Shuba, 2018). This often leads to the overlook of privacy statements and in turn the privacy of the user's data.

Multiple of studies concluded that privacy settings are useful but not sufficient in managing personal data (Bakopoulou, Choffnes, Le, Markopoulou, Mehrabadi and Shuba, 2018; Chen, Chen and Lam, 2008). One paper outlines three ways to stop exposure, these are, privacy settings, getting an owners confirmation of data that will be published about them and browser scope settings, for example, a group of people need to accept someone wanting to join the group( Chen, Chen, and Lam, 2008).

Conclusion:

To conclude, users self-disclose personal information online and often on social media platforms. Social media platforms normally have applications that users instinctually accept to download on the users' phone, the privacy statements are rarely read. These applications can transmit personal information over networks, transmitting data over a network is likely to lead to malicious attacks on data. The technology to track personal data being sent over a network has been developed but needs more time before its publicly useable. In the meantime, privacy settings are useful but will not stop data leakage. Furthermore, any application that allows another user to post data about a user should first seek the approval of the user, the user should be uncomfortable about what others are sharing about them if the application doesn’t have this capability.
